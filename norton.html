INTRODUCTION

The Norton theorem is a fundamental concept in electrical engineering that allows us to simplify complex circuits and analyze their behavior. The Norton theorem states that any complex electrical network consisting of linear, passive components can be replaced by an equivalent circuit consisting of a current source in parallel with a resistor. The Norton equivalent circuit has the same total current and voltage as the original network, and it simplifies the analysis of the circuit by reducing the number of components and connections.

The Norton theorem is named after its inventor, Edward Lawry Norton, who developed this concept in the early 20th century. The Norton theorem is closely related to the Thevenin theorem, which states that any complex electrical network can be replaced by an equivalent circuit consisting of a voltage source in series with a resistor.

THEORY

Norton's Theorem:

According to Norton's theorem any linear* bilateral* network irrespective of its complexity, can be simplified into a Norton's equivalent circuit consisting of a Norton's short circuit current 'IN' in parallel with Norton's equivalent resistance 'RN' in parallel with load resistor RL.




[Fig.1: Norton's equivalent circuit]



Steps to solve :

  1. Identify the load resistor RL.
  2. Remove the load resistor RL and replace all the active sources by its internal resistances. The equivalent resistance between the two open ends will be the Norton's resistance RN.
  3. Again Replace RL with a short circuit branch. And The current flowing through this short circuit branch will be the Nortons's current IN.
  4. Draw the Norton's equivalent circuit and use the identity to Calculate IL.
$$ {I_L= \frac{I_N\times R_N}{R_N+R_L}} $$

Note :

A linear circuit is that whose output is linearly related (or directly proportional) to its input.

A bilateral circuit is that which exhibits its properties equally in either direction.

A short circuit is a circuit element with resistance approaching zero.

An open circuit is a current element with resistance approaching infinity.
